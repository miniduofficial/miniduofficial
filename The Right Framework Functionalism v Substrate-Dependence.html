The Right Framework

Functionalism v Substrate-Dependence

A central question in the philosophy of mind concerns whether the biological substrate underlying cognition contributes essentially to the emergence of mental processes such as intelligence or consciousness, or whether these phenomena depend solely on functional organisation. That is, whether cognition is nothing but a system that transitions between states based on input and then gives an output dependent on the internal state and the input. If the latter is the case, then it is plausible that intelligence, and perhaps consciousness, could emerge in systems implemented in substrates other than biological neural tissue. However, this notion presupposes that cognitive processes can be understood as forms of computation, and that the mechanisms responsible for intelligence and consciousness can be described computationally. If this is the case, then cognitive properties should be preserved under computational equivalence, regardless of the physical substrate in which they are realised.

A formidable counter-argument to the functionalist position is Searle's famous Chinese room argument. Suppose that there exists a room occupied by a person who does not understand Chinese to any degree. The room has a window through which he is given some Chinese symbols, and he has a rulebook based on which he is supposed to arrange these symbols such that they contain responses in Chinese to questions asked in Chinese. He then sends these Chinese responses out of the room through another window. For an external observer, the room can converse in Chinese; but the man does not speak nor understand Chinese to any degree. Searle's position is that syntax is not sufficient for semantics. In a sense, you cannot abstract away the functions of cognition and hope that the system implemented from that blueprint is thereby sentient.
